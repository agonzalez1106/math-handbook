Numbers & Operations
Place Value & Rounding

Every digit in a number has a place, and each place is worth 10 times more than the place to its right.

A digit one place to the LEFT is worth 10 times more. So the 8 in 589,310 (ten-thousands) is 10× the 8 in 598,301 (thousands).
★ Example

Round 353,795 to the nearest ten-thousand.

1️⃣ Find the ten-thousands digit: 353,795 → the 5 is in the ten-thousands spot.

2️⃣ Look at the next digit to the right (the 3 in thousands).

3️⃣ Less than 5? Round DOWN. 5 or more? Round UP. The 3 says round down.

✅ Answer: 350,000

★ Memory Trick

"5 or more, raise the score. 4 or less, let it rest."

Numbers & Operations
Comparing Big Numbers

Use these symbols to compare numbers:

The "alligator" eats the BIGGER number. The open mouth always faces the larger one!
★ How to compare 46,572 and 37,512
  1. Line them up by place value
  2. Start from the LEFT (biggest place)
  3. Compare digit by digit until they're different

46,572 vs 37,512 → 4 is bigger than 3, so 46,572 > 37,512

Numbers & Operations
Big Multiplication

For multi-digit multiplication like 48 × 28, use the area model or standard algorithm.

Break each number into its place values, multiply each piece, then add them all up!
★ Pro Tip

Always estimate first! 48 × 28 is close to 50 × 30 = 1,500. So your answer should be near 1,500. ✓ 1,344 makes sense!

Numbers & Operations
Division & Remainders

When you divide and there's something left over, that's the remainder.

★ Example: 439 ÷ 7

How many groups of 7 fit into 439?

7 × 60 = 420 (close, but we have 19 left)

7 × 2 = 14 (now 19 − 14 = 5 left)

So 439 ÷ 7 = 62 remainder 5 🌱 5 plants left over!

★ Watch Out!

Read the question carefully — it changes what to do with the remainder:

  • "How many left over?" → answer is the remainder
  • "How many vans needed?" → round UP (you need an extra van for the leftovers!)
  • "How many full bags?" → ignore the remainder
★ Van Problem

38 people ÷ 8 per van = 4 remainder 6.

You can't leave 6 people behind! You need 5 vans (4 full + 1 for the extras).

Word Problems
Multi-Step Word Problems

Some problems have more than one step. Slow down and tackle one piece at a time.

★ Marisol's Order

4 tacos at $4 each + 9 tamales at $3 each + 1 horchata for $3. Paid with $50. How much change?

  1. Tacos: 4 × $4 = $16
  2. Tamales: 9 × $3 = $27
  3. Horchata: $3
  4. Total cost: $16 + $27 + $3 = $46
  5. Change: $50 − $46 = $4 ✓
Underline what the question is REALLY asking. Then plan your steps before you start calculating.

Word Problems
"Times As Many" Comparisons

When you see "times as many" or "times as much," that means MULTIPLY.

★ Michael's Money

Michael earned $27 running errands. He earned $12 LESS than that washing windows. He earned 3 TIMES AS MUCH walking dogs as he earned washing windows.

  1. Errands = $27
  2. Windows = $27 − $12 = $15
  3. Dogs = 3 × $15 = $45 ✓
★ Tricky Chains!

If Erika has 6 postcards, Tanya has 2× Erika, and Tanya has 4× Nathan... work it step by step:

  • Tanya = 2 × 6 = 12
  • Tanya = 4 × Nathan, so Nathan = 12 ÷ 4 = 3

Numbers & Operations
Factors & Multiples
Factors are numbers you multiply TO GET a number. Multiples are what you GET when you multiply BY a number.
★ Factors of 12

What numbers multiply to make 12? 1 × 12, 2 × 6, 3 × 4

So factors of 12 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

★ Multiples of 4

Count by 4s: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 28... (the times-4 list!)

★ Even Number Multiples

Multiples of any EVEN number are ALWAYS EVEN. So 33 cannot be a multiple of 2, 4, 6, or 8 — it's odd!

Algebraic Thinking
Number Patterns

Find the rule, then apply it to find the next number — or a faraway number.

★ Pattern: 7, 14, 21, 28, 35...

Rule: "Add 7" (or just count by 7s — multiples of 7!).

What's the 20th number? It's 7 × 20 = 140.

★ Shortcut

If a pattern starts at 7 and adds 7 each time, the Nth number is N × 7. The 20th is 20 × 7 = 140!

Fractions
Equivalent Fractions

Equivalent fractions are different fractions that show the SAME amount.

To make an equivalent fraction, multiply (or divide) the top AND bottom by the same number.
★ Example

1/2 × 3/3 = 3/6 — so 1/2 and 3/6 are equivalent!

10
Fractions
Comparing Fractions

To compare fractions with different denominators (like 6/8 vs 7/12), give them the same bottom number.

★ Compare 6/8 and 7/12

Common denominator = 24

6/8 = 18/24   (×3 top and bottom)

7/12 = 14/24   (×2 top and bottom)

18/24 > 14/24, so 6/8 > 7/12 ✓

★ Benchmark Trick

Compare each fraction to 1/2. 6/8 is more than half (4/8 = 1/2). 7/12 is just over half (6/12 = 1/2). 6/8 = 9/12, which is way more than 7/12!

11
Fractions
Adding & Subtracting Fractions
When fractions have the SAME denominator, just add or subtract the TOPS. The bottom stays the same!
★ Example: 5/4 − 3/4

The bottoms match! 5/4 − 3/4 = 2/4 hour

★ Mixed Numbers: 2 3/8 + 3 4/8

You can change them to improper fractions first:

2 3/8 = 19/8    3 4/8 = 28/8

19/8 + 28/8 = 47/8

Or add the wholes and fractions separately: 5 + 7/8 = 5 7/8

★ Subtracting from a Whole: 1 − 6/8

Rewrite 1 as 8/8. Then 8/8 − 6/8 = 2/8 ✓

12
Fractions
Fraction × Whole Number

Multiplying a fraction by a whole number = adding the fraction to itself that many times.

★ Example: 4 × 2/3

= 2/3 + 2/3 + 2/3 + 2/3

= 8/3

Shortcut: multiply the top by the whole number → (4 × 2)/3 = 8/3

Geometry
Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is any 4-sided shape. They have a "family tree" — some are special types of others!

A square is a rectangle AND a rhombus AND a parallelogram. A rectangle is a parallelogram. Going DOWN the family tree adds rules; going UP, the rules disappear.
★ Tricky Test Question

"Are figures 2 and 3 the only rectangles?" If figure 1 looks like a square (a tilted square = "diamond"), it IS still a rectangle! Squares count as rectangles.

14
Geometry
Lines of Symmetry

A line of symmetry is a line you could fold on so both halves match exactly.

★ Common Mistake

A diagonal line on a regular (non-square) RECTANGLE is NOT a line of symmetry. The two halves don't match if you fold!

15
Geometry
Angles
Angles can be ADDED together! If ∠AOB = 40°, ∠BOC = 60°, and ∠COD = 20°, then ∠AOD = 40 + 60 + 20 = 120°.
★ Angles in a Circle

A full circle = 360°. So a 20° angle turns through 20 one-degree angles (20 of the 360 little wedges in a circle).

16
Geometry
Triangles

Triangles are named by their biggest angle:

  • Acute triangle: ALL three angles less than 90° (e.g., 60°, 60°, 60°)
  • Right triangle: ONE angle exactly 90° (e.g., 30°, 60°, 90°)
  • Obtuse triangle: ONE angle MORE than 90° (e.g., 30°, 40°, 110°)
All three angles of a triangle ALWAYS add up to 180°.
★ Quick Check

30° + 40° + 110° = 180° ✓ AND 110° > 90°, so this is an obtuse triangle.

17
Measurement
Area & Perimeter
PERIMETER = distance AROUND the shape (add all sides).
AREA = space INSIDE the shape (length × width for rectangles).
★ Working Backwards

"Area = 6 sq ft, length = 3 ft. What's the perimeter?"

Width = 6 ÷ 3 = 2 ft. Perimeter = 3 + 2 + 3 + 2 = 10 ft ✓

★ Same Area, Different Perimeter

10 × 4 = 40 sq in (area). What other rectangle has area 40? 8 × 5 = 40 ✓ — same area, but perimeter = 26 (different!).

Data
Line Plots with Fractions

A line plot uses Xs above a number line. Each X = one data point.

★ Total Hours from the Plot

1 X at 1/4 + 2 Xs at 2/4 + 2 Xs at 3/4

= 1/4 + 2/4 + 2/4 + 3/4 + 3/4

= 11/4 hours ✓

★ Reading Carefully

Count the Xs at each spot. To find the TOTAL, add up (X count × the fraction at that spot) for every spot.
